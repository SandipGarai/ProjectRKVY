Know Your Farmer, Know Your Consumer (KYFKYC)

Development of an E-Marketplace Platform with Farmer Recommender System

1. Introduction

The KYFKYC initiative seeks to bridge the gap between farmers and consumers by establishing a direct, transparent, and efficient e-marketplace. This platform will eliminate intermediaries, ensure farmers receive fair prices for their produce, and enable consumers to access fresh, locally sourced products with full traceability to the source. By integrating a robust recommender system, the project will also guide farmers towards optimal farming practices, market-driven cropping patterns, and resource optimization.

2. Objectives

i. Establish a direct farmerconsumer e-marketplace, reducing dependency on middlemen.

ii. Create a transparent trading environment where farmers sell directly to consumers at fair prices.

iii. Develop a data-driven recommender system to advise farmers on farming patterns, resource use, and solutions to their specific challenges.

iv. Generate rural employment opportunities through data recording, order management, transportation, and packaging.

v. Facilitate farmer cooperatives and partnerships to share resources, responsibilities, and marketing channels.

vi. Support the creation of post-harvest value addition facilities for region-specific products.

vii. Promote traceability, allowing consumers to verify the origin of their purchased goods.

viii. Encourage sustainable agriculture, rural economic growth, and farmer empowerment.

ix. Replicate the model in other regions to promote agricultural entrepreneurship and rural development.

3. Problem Statement

Currently, consumers often face challenges in sourcing specific agricultural products, while farmers struggle to market small-volume produce. Due to the lack of direct sales channels, farmers sell to middlemen at wholesale prices that are often significantly lower than market rates. Consumers, in turn, pay higher retail prices without knowing the origin or quality of the produce. This disconnect results in reduced farmer profitability and limited consumer choice.

4. Concept Overview

The KYFKYC platform will:

  • Enable consumers to view available products, their source villages, and individual farmers.
  • Allow consumers to place orders directly with their preferred farmers.
  • Provide farmers with real-time demand visibility.
  • Record farmer and product data for market analysis and personalized recommendations.

5. Example Implementation Scenario

Geographical Setup

  • Five villages within a 10 km radius.
  • One central main station and four substations (one in each producing village).

Sample Data: Marketable Products by Village

  • Village 1: P1 20 farmers, P2 30 farmers, P3 40 farmers
  • Village 2: P1 40, P4 50, P5 60, P6 10
  • Village 3: P2 10, P4 20, P7 30
  • Village 4: P8 90, P9 15, P10 20
  • Village 5: Consumption only (urban center)

6. Operational Workflow

Step 1: Farmer Input

  • By 7:00 PM daily, each farmer updates the substation (or directly via mobile app) on the quantity of produce available for sale the next morning.
  • Farmers without mobile devices can report directly at the substation.

Step 2: Data Aggregation

  • Data from all substations is uploaded to the KYFKYC application.
  • Consumers can browse available products by farmer, village, and distance.

Step 3: Consumer Ordering

  • Consumers place orders before nightfall.
  • Orders may be based on product quality, past purchase experience, or farmer reputation.
  • Example: Farmer F1 in Village 1 receives orders for 2 kg P1 and 4 kg P2 from three different consumers, including one who prefers his produce over local availability due to prior satisfaction.

Step 4: FarmerConsumer Relationship

  • Over time, consumers build loyalty towards certain farmers, and farmers become aware of their regular customers.
  • This branding effect strengthens trust and reduces marketing costs.

Step 5: Packaging & Delivery

  • Farmers pack and label products separately for each customer.
  • Substations coordinate van transport to consumer villages.
  • Delivery teams consist of a driver and a loader/unloader.

7. Data Utilization and Farmer Support

Data Collection

  • Daily farmer sales records maintained at substations.
  • Additional details collected over time:
    • Cultivated area, soil type, irrigation source, electricity access
    • Labor availability, cultivation challenges
    • Investment vs. return analysis

Recommender System Functionality

  • Combine weather, soil, and sales data to provide crop recommendations.
  • Advise on optimal cropping patterns based on market demand, available resources, and farmer capacity.
  • Identify surplus and shortage trends for better market alignment.

8. Employment Generation

  • Substation data operators
  • Delivery drivers and logistics staff
  • Packaging assistants (family or hired labor)
  • Value addition unit workers

9. Collaboration and Value Addition

  • Farmers producing the same crop can be connected for joint marketing and shared input procurement.
  • Identification of dominant regional crops for targeted post-harvest processing units.
  • Traceability features will allow consumers to track produce back to the specific farmer and village.

10. Expected Outcomes

  • Fairer farmer incomes
  • Reduced consumer prices
  • Stronger farmerconsumer relationships
  • Improved market transparency
  • Increased rural employment
  • Better crop planning and resource use through data-driven insights
